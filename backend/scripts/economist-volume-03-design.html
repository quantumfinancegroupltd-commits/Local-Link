From the Editor
05
The Hands Ghana Has Forgotten
Letter from the Editor-in-Chief

In the courtyard of a vocational training centre in Tamale, I watched a young woman named Fatima carefully thread a conduit through a concrete wall. She was twenty-two years old, four months into her electrical installation apprenticeship, and faster and more precise than any of the three men beside her. Her trainer told me she was the best student he had seen in six years of teaching. He also told me that when she finishes and goes to find work, she will almost certainly be paid less than they will.

This is the story of women in Ghana's skilled trades. Not a story of absence — because women have always been part of the trades, in tailoring, in food processing, in hairdressing. It is a story of invisible ceilings: the trades where women are allowed to work, the wages they are allowed to earn, and the training systems that have for decades been designed around male learners and male career paths.

This third edition of LocalLink Economist is devoted entirely to understanding that story through data. We surveyed 62 women in skilled trades across six regions. We collected wage data for 18 trade occupations, disaggregated by gender. We analysed five years of TVET enrolment records and NAP apprenticeship data. We tracked employment and income outcomes for female completers of technical programmes. And we spent two weeks in the field, listening.

What we found is both encouraging and sobering. The encouraging part: female TVET enrolment has grown 61% since 2020. Thirty-four thousand women are currently registered in the National Apprenticeship Programme. Women who complete technical qualifications and find employment in their trade earn incomes 2.3 times higher than women who enter the informal economy without qualifications. The trades work for women — when women can get into them.

The sobering part: women remain 18% of formal TVET enrolment. In construction trades specifically, they are 4%. The gender pay gap across all skilled trades averages 23% — even for identical qualifications and identical experience levels. Dropout rates for women in technical programmes are 1.8 times higher than for men, driven overwhelmingly by financial hardship, childcare obligations, and hostile training environments. These are not cultural inevitabilities. They are policy failures.

We dedicate this edition to Fatima, to Grace, to Abena, to all the women who showed us their hands and their workshops and their certificates and said: this is what I can do. The question for Ghana's policymakers is whether they are listening.

Kwame A. Mensah
Editor-in-Chief, LocalLink Economist
Accra, March 2026

The Pattern Is Consistent
In every male-dominated skilled trade, women earn a median daily rate 17–26% below their male counterparts for equivalent qualifications and experience. The gap is widest in construction (24–25%) and electrical trades (20–26%). The pattern strongly suggests discrimination in client and employer pricing rather than skill or productivity differences.
Female-Dominated Trades Show Near Parity
In hairdressing, dressmaking, and catering — trades where women are the majority — gender pay gaps have compressed to 5–6%. This suggests that female market power, not inherent trade structure, determines pay equity. The solution to the pay gap in male-dominated trades is, in part, more women in them.

TVET Enrolment Analysis — 2020 to 2025
14
Data Analysis
Five Years of Progress — And Why 18% Is Not Enough
Female TVET enrolment has grown impressively since 2020, but a closer look at the data reveals a system still sorted by gender — and a dropout problem that erases much of the gain before it reaches the labour market.
By Ama Boateng, Lead Data Analyst & Priscilla Aidoo, Quantitative Research Lead

The headline number is encouraging. Between 2020 and 2025, the number of women enrolled in formal TVET programmes in Ghana rose from 46,200 to 74,382 — a 61% increase over five years. On its own, that is a genuine achievement. The National Apprenticeship Programme, the expansion of community TVET centres in the Northern regions, and — critically — deliberate outreach by a number of COTVET-affiliated institutions have all contributed.

But aggregate enrolment numbers hide a pattern that should give pause. Of those 74,382 female TVET students in 2025, approximately 82% are enrolled in just four programme categories: hairdressing and cosmetology, dressmaking and fashion design, catering and hospitality, and business studies. These are programmes that are culturally coded as female — which is not a criticism of the programmes themselves, but a reflection of a system that has not seriously challenged the sorting of women and men into different economic destinies at the age of fifteen or sixteen.

The Dropout Crisis

The second number that demands attention is dropout. Among women enrolled in TVET programmes across all categories, the dropout rate in 2025 was 31% — compared to 17% for men. The primary reasons cited by women who leave programmes early: financial hardship (68% of dropouts), childcare obligations with no provision in the training centre (52%), and hostile or uncomfortable training environments (34%). Only 12% cited academic difficulty.

This is a system design problem, not a learner problem. TVET stipends for female students in technical programmes average GH₵ 380 per month — less than half the cost of accommodation in most urban training centres. There is no childcare provision in any public TVET centre in Ghana. And training environments in male-dominated trades are, by the account of women who have navigated them, frequently unwelcoming at best and hostile at worst.

What the Regional Data Shows

The regional breakdown of female technical enrolment reveals a geographic dimension to the gender gap in TVET. Greater Accra leads all regions in absolute female enrolment but performs poorly on female share of technical (non-service) programmes — reflecting a large overall system with persistent gender sorting. The Northern Region, despite lower absolute numbers, has seen the fastest growth in female enrolment in technical trades, driven partly by community apprenticeship models that are more accommodating of women's domestic obligations.

The Upper East Region stands out for a different reason: a higher-than-national-average completion rate for women who do enrol — 74%, compared to the national female completion rate of 69%. Researchers attribute this partly to the strong community accountability structures in traditional apprenticeship contexts and to the relatively lower cost-of-living pressures compared to urban centres.

Adum, Kumasi · March 2026
Feature Portrait
"They Ask for the Man in Charge. Then They Watch Me Work."
Grace Sarpong, 28, licensed electrician, Kumasi. Six years in the trade. One business. One rule: she doesn't work for less than the men.
By Efua Quartey, Lead Correspondent

The workshop is a ground-floor room off a courtyard on Adum's market street, and it smells of solder and the electrical cable insulation that hangs in loose coils along one wall. Grace Sarpong arrives from a site job still in her work trousers and safety boots, apologises for the fifteen-minute delay, and immediately begins answering questions with the directness of someone who has spent six years not being heard and has decided to compensate.

She grew up in Akim Oda, the fifth of seven children, and came to Kumasi at seventeen to live with an aunt. The electrical apprenticeship was not the plan — it was the opportunity. A neighbour was a licensed electrician. He needed a helper. She was available. "He thought I would quit after two weeks," she says. "I didn't quit."

Four years of informal apprenticeship, followed by two years of evening technical study, produced an ECGB licence in 2022. By then she had already been working independently for a year, relying on the networks her former employer had built and her own reputation, which spread largely by word of mouth among women who wanted an electrician they didn't feel condescended to.

"Male clients sometimes ask if I've called the man in charge," she says. "I tell them I am the person in charge. Then I fix whatever they called about and usually they don't ask again." She charges GH₵ 120 per day — the Kumasi market rate for a licensed electrician. She has never accepted less. "The first time a client offered me GH₵ 80 because I was a woman, I said no and walked away. Word got around that I didn't discount. That's the best marketing I ever did."

"I don't charge less because I'm a woman. If I did, I'd be telling every other woman in this trade that it's acceptable to be paid less. It isn't."
— Grace Sarpong, Licensed Electrician, Kumasi

Her second employee, Isaac, came to her two years ago after working for a large electrical contractor for four years. "He said he wanted to learn how to run his own business," Grace says. "I told him that was fine, but first he had to learn to listen to a woman — because in this workshop, I am the person in charge." He agreed. He is, by her account, a careful and reliable worker.

The challenge of managing male employees is one that comes up repeatedly in conversations with women who have built businesses in the trades. The social scripts are not designed for female bosses in manual occupations. "Some clients still call Isaac to confirm what I've said," Grace admits. "He always tells them exactly what I told them. And then we have a laugh about it afterwards."

The Pricing Stand

Grace's refusal to price below the market rate is not simply principle — it is strategy. She describes it as one of the most important business decisions she has made. "If I charge GH₵ 80 when the rate is GH₵ 120, I am not competing. I am subsidising the prejudice. I am telling the market that a woman's work is worth less. Every time a woman does that, it becomes harder for the next woman to charge the right rate."

She has had jobs go elsewhere because of it. Three times in 2023, she gave a quote that matched the male-market rate and did not get the work. In each case, she later found the client had hired a cheaper alternative — and in two of the three cases, had subsequently called her back to fix what the cheaper alternative had done incorrectly. "I charged full rate to fix it," she says. "They paid."

The ECGB licence — the Electrical Contractors and General Builders registration that qualifies her to take commercial and residential electrical installation contracts — was the turning point. Without it, she was taking small household jobs. With it, she could tender for contracts with property developers, hotels, and commercial builders. Her revenue in 2025 was approximately GH₵ 43,000 — equivalent to about GH₵ 3,580 per month, nearly double the national median for female skilled trade workers.

What She Would Change

If she could change one thing about the training system, it would be the pathway to certification. "I spent two years studying in the evening after full days on site," she says. "The course cost me GH₵ 4,200 that I didn't have. I borrowed it. There is no stipend for women doing evening technical courses. There is no subsidy for the licensing examination fee. The system assumes you have money and time. Most women in the trades don't have either."

She has mentored three women through the early stages of electrical apprenticeships since 2023 — providing informal advice, site visits, and the kind of professional network introduction that male tradespeople give each other without thinking. "No one gave me a network," she says. "I am trying to be the network for someone else."

Feature — The Sunyani Welders

Cooperative Profile
The Twelve Who Built a Guild
In 2021, twelve women who had completed the NAP welding programme in Sunyani found they could not get hired individually. So they stopped trying individually.
By Yaw Owusu, Features Writer
Members of the Sunyani Women Welders Cooperative at their shared workshop, Sunyani New Town — January 2026. Photo: Nana Addo Boateng.

When Akosua Poku finished her NAP welding certificate in mid-2021, she went looking for work at the nine fabrication shops in Sunyani. None would hire her. The reasons given varied — "we don't have changing facilities," "clients would be uncomfortable," "the other workers wouldn't accept it" — but the outcome was consistent. After eight rejections, she called the other women from her cohort. They had the same story.

"We had twelve women with certificates and no jobs," she says. "We couldn't find an employer who would take us. So we became the employer." The Sunyani Women Welders Cooperative was formally registered in November 2021 with capital of GH₵ 14,400 — GH₵ 1,200 each, much of it borrowed — and a shared workshop space rented for GH₵ 800 per month in Sunyani New Town.

Building a Client Base

The first year was hard. The cooperative's initial market — household gate and grille fabrication — was dominated by established male-owned shops. The women's strategy was not to compete on price but on quality and reliability. They standardised their welding specifications, introduced a written quotation system (unusual in the local market), and offered a 12-month warranty on structural welds. Within six months, a property developer in Sunyani was placing regular orders.

By the end of 2022, the cooperative had 18 regular commercial clients, including two government institutions and a construction contractor based in Accra who subcontracts gate and security grille fabrication across Brong-Ahafo. Annual turnover in 2025 was GH₵ 486,000 — up from GH₵ 92,000 in their first full year. Each of the twelve founding members earned an average of GH₵ 2,800 per month in 2025, well above the Sunyani skilled trade median of GH₵ 1,950.

What They Changed

The cooperative has had a measurable effect on the local skills ecosystem. Two of the twelve members now run NAP mentorship programmes, and fourteen young women from Sunyani have enrolled in welding programmes since 2022 — citing the cooperative as their reason. The Bono Region TVET Service has used the cooperative as a case study in its gender-inclusion training for institutional employers.

Construction Trades — Sector Analysis
40
Sector Analysis
Construction: The Last Frontier
With women at just 4% of construction trade enrolment and less than 1% of registered construction workers, the building sector remains the most resistant to female entry — yet the structural barriers are far more surmountable than cultural narratives suggest.
By Adjoa Asante-Twum, TVET & Skills Policy Correspondent

The construction sector has a gender problem that is, at its core, an infrastructure problem. When women visit TVET institutions to enquire about construction programmes, they encounter facilities that were designed entirely without female users in mind: no female-only changing rooms, no female sanitary facilities on training sites, physical environments built around male body norms and male social dynamics. These are not insurmountable barriers — they are design failures that can be fixed with money and will.

They are also, it must be said, compounded by cultural assumptions that have been hardened by decades of male-only practice. Contractors report that female applicants face discouragement at every stage: from family members who question the choice, from TVET trainers who have lower expectations, and from employers who simply do not consider them. The Ghana Contractors Association has fewer than 200 registered female members out of more than 8,000 total — a ratio of 2.5%.

Where Women Are Entering

The picture is not uniformly bleak. Women are entering construction at higher rates in three specific niches: tiling and floor finishes (22% female, rising from 8% in 2018), interior painting and decoration (14%, rising from 5%), and project management and quantity surveying (19% of new QS graduates are women, up from 6% in 2015). These niches share a common characteristic — they are viewed as "clean" or "technical" rather than physically demanding, making social gatekeeping less intense.

There is evidence that this entry point matters. Several of the most successful female construction entrepreneurs in Accra entered through tiling or painting, built client relationships and management skills, and expanded into broader contracting. The path exists — but it requires exceptional individual persistence to navigate because the system does not facilitate it.

The Physical Barrier Myth

One argument consistently deployed against female entry into construction trades is physical capacity — the claim that construction work requires upper-body strength that women typically don't possess. This argument does not survive contact with evidence. Modern construction techniques — power tools, mechanical lifting, concrete pumping — have dramatically reduced the role of brute strength in most trades. More importantly, trades like electrical installation, plumbing, tiling, plastering, and painting have never required exceptional physical strength, yet remain male-dominated. Physical capacity is a rationalisation for social preference, not a genuine barrier.

The Workshop Floor: Automotive, Fabrication & Manufacturing
Once almost entirely male, Ghana's automotive repair and light manufacturing sectors are seeing a quiet but measurable shift — driven by a new generation of women who trained in polytechnic programmes and are now refusing to be invisible on the workshop floor.
By Yaw Owusu, Features Writer & Ama Boateng, Data Analyst

Ghana's automotive repair sector employs an estimated 340,000 workers across formal workshops, roadside mechanics' bays, and the large urban clusters — most notably Suame Magazine in Kumasi and Kokompe in Accra. Women have historically represented less than 2% of this workforce. As of 2025, that figure has risen to approximately 3.2% — modest in absolute terms, but representing a 60% increase in the female automotive workforce since 2020 and a genuine shift in who considers the trade accessible.

The change is not evenly distributed. In the formal sector — authorised dealerships, fleet maintenance contractors, and workshop chains with structured employment contracts — female mechanics and auto-electricians now represent 8–12% of intake, driven by polytechnic and HND programmes that have actively recruited women since 2021. In the informal roadside sector, the female presence remains negligible. The training pipeline is improving; the informal market's social barriers are not moving at the same pace.

The Diagnostic Advantage

One specific shift is worth noting. Modern vehicle diagnostics — the use of OBD-II scanners, oscilloscopes, and manufacturer software to identify faults — has fundamentally changed what auto mechanics do. The old trade was primarily mechanical: physical disassembly and reassembly, often requiring significant upper-body strength for suspension and drivetrain work. The new trade is increasingly electronic and analytical: reading data, interpreting fault codes, understanding engine management systems.

Women trained in modern automotive programmes are disproportionately represented in diagnostic specialisms. Esi Amoah (profiled on page 20) built her entire business model around Toyota and Hyundai diagnostics — and built an 80% female clientele in the process. Her observation that female clients come to her specifically to avoid condescension points to a market dynamic that the industry has not caught up with: women are a growing segment of Ghana's vehicle ownership base, and they prefer mechanics who treat them as capable adults.

"The car doesn't care who plugs in the diagnostic scanner. The data is the data. This is the first trade where being new to it is actually an advantage — there are no old rules to unlearn."
— Lecturer, Automotive Technology, Accra Technical Training Centre
Light Manufacturing: Aluminium & Metal Fabrication

Ghana's light manufacturing sector — aluminium window and door fabrication, metal furniture, roofing sheet production, PVC fitting — has historically employed women primarily in finishing and quality control roles. The physically demanding cutting, bending, and welding operations have been male by default rather than by design. TVET programme data for 2025 shows 18% female enrolment in aluminium fabrication — the fastest-growing female share of any workshop trade, up from 6% in 2020.

The growth is partly explained by the economics. Aluminium fabrication is one of the better-paid workshop trades in Ghana, with licensed fabricators earning GH₵ 98–130 per day. As more women have entered the polytechnic programmes that produce fabricators, the economic case has been made directly to the families and communities who influence young women's training choices. Earnings speak where arguments about gender equality do not always reach.

POLICY
& OUTLOOK
What must change — in TVET design, procurement rules, pay equity, and the systems that shape women's access to skilled work
Policy Interview
Ms. Comfort Asante
Director, TVET Service, Ministry of Education, Ghana
On gender-responsive TVET, the NAP apprenticeship programme, and what reform must deliver — Interviewed by Adjoa Asante-Twum
Female TVET enrolment is up 61% since 2020. Is this a success story?
It is progress, and I don't want to dismiss it. The work done by TVET institutions and the COTVET NAP programme to reach more women has been real and difficult. But when we disaggregate that 61% growth, we find that almost all of it is in programme categories that were already female-dominated — hairdressing, catering, fashion. The share of women in technical and engineering programmes has grown, but from a very low base. We went from 4% female enrolment in electrical installation to 16%. That's fourfold growth — but it's still 16%. No one building a gender-equitable skills system should be satisfied with 16%.
The dropout rate for women in TVET is 31%, compared to 17% for men. What is driving that, and what can the system do about it?
We know exactly what is driving it. The research is unambiguous. Women drop out because they cannot afford to stay enrolled — stipends are inadequate, accommodation costs are high, and many women have dependants. They drop out because there are no childcare provisions. And they drop out because the training environment in some programmes is not safe or welcoming for them. These are all fixable problems. The first requires money — we need to double the female stipend in technical programmes, full stop. The second requires infrastructure investment — every public TVET centre should have crèche provision. The third requires a culture change that starts with trainer selection and training. None of this is rocket science. It's a question of whether it's a priority.
The gender pay gap in skilled trades averages 23% even for equivalent qualifications. Is TVET reform enough, or does pay equity require different interventions?
TVET reform addresses the supply side — getting more qualified women into the market. The pay gap is a demand-side problem. It reflects employer and client preferences — the willingness to pay a woman less for the same work. Two things can change that. First, more women in the trades — when women are a larger share of the qualified workforce, the market can't systematically underpay them without losing access to them. Second, public sector procurement can set the standard. Government contracts should require equal pay as a condition of award. If the government is your biggest construction client and it requires equal pay, the market shifts. We don't have that yet, but it's a policy lever that costs nothing to implement.

Opinion
By Dr. Akosua Darkwah
Department of Sociology, University of Ghana
The Stipend Is Not the Problem. The System Is.
Ghana's TVET reform agenda is right about the solutions and wrong about the causes. Until we understand why women are excluded from skilled trades, we cannot fix it.
Dr. Akosua Darkwah  ·  Dept. of Sociology, University of Ghana  ·  Legon

Every six months, someone publishes a report on women and TVET in Ghana that recommends increasing stipends, building female toilets, and running awareness campaigns. These are good recommendations. They are also insufficient — and the reason they are insufficient is that they treat a structural problem as an individual or logistical one.

The question we should be asking is not: what practical barriers prevent women from enrolling in TVET? The question is: why, in a society where women perform the majority of physical and manual labour, have the skilled trades been constructed as male space? The answer to that question is not about toilets or stipends. It is about how economic value has been systematically assigned to male labour and denied to female labour — and how that assignment has been reproduced, generation after generation, in training systems, labour markets, and the domestic economy.

Consider the asymmetry at the heart of Ghana's gender and trades data. Women at 18% of TVET enrolment is described as a problem. Women at 92% of hairdressing enrolment is not described as a problem. We notice the male-dominated spaces and ask how women can enter them. We do not notice the female-dominated spaces and ask why the work that happens in them is paid less, valued less, and excluded from the "skilled trades" framework that receives policy attention.

Until we are willing to acknowledge that the designation of certain labour as skilled — and therefore deserving of investment, certification, and decent wages — has historically tracked gender rather than actual complexity, we will keep adding women to a system built to extract value from them, rather than building a system that distributes value fairly.

"The real reform is not getting women into the trades as they exist. It is asking what the trades would look like if they had always included women — and building those."
— Dr. Akosua Darkwah

This is not an argument against stipend increases or better sanitation facilities. It is an argument for a deeper reform agenda — one that includes reclassifying hairdressing and dressmaking as skilled trades deserving of the same NVQ framework and wage standards as electrical installation; that includes requiring gender pay audits as a condition of TVET programme certification; that includes designing community apprenticeship standards that recognise and formalise the informal training that women already provide to each other, invisibly and without payment.

Ghana has the data. It has the TVET infrastructure. It has, in the cooperative model and the individual stories in this magazine, proof that women are not the problem and never were. What it needs is the political will to build a system in which their skill is recognised for what it is worth.

Five Policy Priorities for 2026
Concrete, costed, and overdue — what government, TVET institutions, and the private sector must do to turn progress into transformation.
01
Double the Female TVET Stipend in Technical Programmes
The current monthly stipend of GH₵ 380 for women in technical TVET programmes does not cover accommodation in any urban training centre. We recommend an immediate increase to GH₵ 800 per month for women enrolled in programmes classified as Level 3 or above in the NVQ framework. Estimated annual cost: GH₵ 28 million. This is less than 0.4% of the national education budget.
Lead Agency: MoE / COTVET · Timeline: 2026 Budget
02

No public TVET centre in Ghana currently provides crèche or childcare facilities. 52% of female TVET dropouts cite childcare obligations as a contributing reason for leaving. We recommend mandating childcare provision — either on-site or via subsidy to registered community childcare providers — for all TVET institutions with 100+ female students. 03

All PPA-governed construction contracts above GH₵ 500,000 should require contractors to certify that female workers on the project are paid equivalent daily rates to male workers with equivalent qualifications and experience. Impact: immediate signalling to the market that the government does not accept a gender pay gap as a cost of procurement.
Lead Agency: PPA / MoF · Timeline: PPA Act Amendment 2026
04
Extend the NVQ Framework to Hairdressing, Dressmaking and Catering
These three trade categories employ the majority of Ghana's female skilled workers but sit outside the NVQ framework that governs certification, wage benchmarks, and career progression for technical trades. Extending the NVQ to these categories would formalise the skills of 400,000+ women, create career pathways from trade worker to supervisor to trainer, and — critically — bring these occupations into the pay equity framework that public sector procurement requirements would enforce.

The Economic Case — Plainly Stated

Ghana has a labour shortage in skilled trades that is structurally embedded and worsening. The construction sector needs an estimated 120,000 additional qualified skilled workers by 2028 to meet the government's infrastructure targets. The manufacturing sector estimates a shortage of 80,000 technical workers. These are not projections — they are current gaps that are already delaying projects, inflating costs, and reducing the capacity of the economy to build what it has committed to build.

Women are 51% of Ghana's population. They are 18% of the formal TVET system and far less than that of the qualified technical workforce. There is no demographic, technological, or economic reason for this. It is a choice — expressed through system design, cultural practice, and policy neglect — and it is an extraordinarily expensive one. If Ghana raised female participation in technical TVET to 40% by 2030 — a goal that Rwanda has already set — it would add an estimated 180,000 qualified technical workers to the economy over the same period. At median skilled trade earnings of GH₵ 1,820 per month, that represents over GH₵ 3.9 billion in annual household income that currently doesn't exist.

This is not a social justice argument, though the social justice case is compelling. It is an economic argument. Ghana needs the workers. Women can be those workers. The barriers that prevent them are man-made and removable.

Business Analysis
The Capital Gap: Why Women Can't Scale
Getting the qualification is hard. Getting hired is harder. But the hardest step of all for women in Ghana's skilled trades is getting the capital to move from employee to business owner — the step that most dramatically changes economic outcomes.
By Abena Osei-Kuffour, Deputy Editor, Economics

There is a point in the career of every skilled tradesperson where the most significant income leap becomes possible: the transition from employee or apprentice to independent operator. For men in Ghana's skilled trades, this transition — setting up a small workshop, registering a business, building a client list — typically happens with the help of family networks, informal credit, and the accumulated contacts of several years working under a master craftsperson who passes introductions along. For women, almost none of these pathways are reliably available.

The data is stark. A LocalLink survey of 62 women in skilled trades found that only 14% had accessed any formal financial product to start or grow their trade business. Of those who had sought formal credit, 71% reported being refused by at least one financial institution, with the primary reasons given: lack of collateral (68%), "insufficient business history" (44%), and — in 22% of cases — explicit statements from loan officers that the trade was "not appropriate" for a woman.

Informal credit — from savings groups (susu), family, or money lenders — has been the primary source of startup capital for the majority of the women we interviewed. The Sunyani Welders Cooperative started with GH₵ 14,400 pooled from twelve members, most of it borrowed. Grace Sarpong borrowed the GH₵ 4,200 for her licensing course from a cousin. Abena Mensah in Takoradi funded her first plumbing tools through a susu group with seven other women from her TVET cohort.

The Bank's Mental Model Problem

The formal financial sector's reluctance to lend to women in manual trades is not purely about collateral or credit history. It reflects a mental model problem — the same one that pervades the trades themselves. Bank loan officers, in interview after interview, expressed surprise at the concept of a female plumber or welder seeking a business loan. The cognitive framework that makes female tradespeople invisible in the labour market makes them invisible to credit officers too.

Several women reported being offered smaller loan amounts than requested without explanation. Two reported being directed to the women's enterprise loan products — micro-finance products designed for market traders and artisanal producers — rather than the business development loans available to trade contractors. The mental model is coherent: women do micro-enterprise, men do trade contracting. Breaking that model requires either individual persistence or system change.

What Finance Products Actually Exist

The formal financial products available to women in Ghana's skilled trades are limited but not zero. The MASLOC (Microfinance and Small Loans Centre) programme has a women's enterprise strand that can provide working capital for registered businesses. Several rural and community banks in the Ashanti and Brong-Ahafo regions have begun offering trade-specific business loans following advocacy from COTVET. The GIZ-supported Skills Development Fund has piloted women's cooperative lending at below-market rates.

None of these reach the majority of women in the trades. Awareness is low, application requirements are complex, and the underlying premise of most products — that women in manual trades are micro-entrepreneurs rather than small business operators — means the loan sizes are often inadequate for what women actually need to grow.

The Informal Pipeline — Women's Hidden Skills Economy
90
Special Report
The Hidden Economy: Women Who Train Without Being Counted
Ghana's formal TVET system enrols 74,000 women. But informal apprenticeship — women training women in shared spaces, marketplaces, and home workshops — reaches an estimated ten times that number and receives almost no policy attention.
By Efua Quartey, Lead Correspondent, Gender & Labour
A dressmaking workshop, Makola Market, Accra — February 2026. Photo: Nana Addo Boateng.

In a market stall in Makola, Accra, a woman named Adiza sits with four apprentices around a cutting table. She is teaching them pattern-making. She has no formal qualification as a teacher. Her apprentices have no formal enrolment record. None of them will appear in the TVET Service's annual statistics. And yet what is happening in that stall is, in all functional respects, vocational education and training — the transfer of economically valuable skills from an experienced practitioner to a new generation.

Informal apprenticeship — the traditional system of learning a trade under a master practitioner without formal institutional enrolment — has been the dominant mode of skills transmission in Ghana for generations. For women, it has historically been the only available pathway into the trades that were already coded as female: dressmaking, food preparation, hairdressing, batik and textile production. Research by the Ghana Statistical Service estimates that between 600,000 and 800,000 women are currently in active informal apprenticeships across Ghana — compared to 74,382 in formal TVET.

The Counting Problem

The informal apprenticeship system is invisible to policy for a simple reason: it is not counted. GLSS 8 data provides broad estimates, but the informal trades do not generate the administrative records — enrolment registers, assessment records, certification data — that formal systems produce. As a result, the skills of women in informal apprenticeship cannot be certified, cannot be used to access formal employment, and cannot be built upon through the NVQ progression framework that provides career pathways in the formal system.

This is not an accident. The designation of trades as "formal" — and therefore worthy of the NVQ framework, certification infrastructure, and policy attention — has historically tracked the trades that men dominated. The trades where women were already the majority have remained outside the formal skills architecture. The consequence is that the vast majority of Ghana's skilled female workforce exists in an economic space where their skills are real but officially unrecognised.

The Formalisation Opportunity

There are models for bringing informal apprenticeship into formal recognition frameworks. Kenya's Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL) system allows practitioners with demonstrable competence — regardless of how they acquired it — to sit assessments and receive NVQ equivalent certification. South Africa's SETA system includes informal trade recognition pathways. Both have had mixed results, but both rest on the correct premise: that certification should follow demonstrated competence, not institutional enrolment.

Ghana's COTVET has a Recognition of Prior Learning policy on paper. In practice, it is almost inaccessible to women in informal trades. The assessment fees are prohibitive (GH₵ 150–400 per level), the assessment centres are urban and inaccessible to rural practitioners, and — critically — the trades where women are concentrated (dressmaking, hairdressing, catering) sit outside the NVQ framework that RPL feeds into, making the pathway circular and pointless. Fixing RPL for women requires fixing the NVQ coverage gap first.

What Formalisation Could Deliver
If 20% of women currently in informal apprenticeship gained RPL certification over five years — 120,000 to 160,000 women — and if those certifications were in an NVQ framework that carried wage floor protection, the income effect at median skilled trade wages would represent approximately GH₵ 2.6 billion in additional annual household income. This is not a marginal intervention. It is one of the highest-return skills policy actions available to the Ghanaian government at relatively low institutional cost.

Akosua Poku
Founding Member & Chair, Sunyani Women Welders Cooperative — Brong-Ahafo Region
On building the cooperative, the first year of failure, and what other women need to know — Interviewed by Yaw Owusu
You've described the cooperative's founding as coming from desperation rather than vision. How do you see it now, four years later?
In the beginning it was pure desperation — twelve women with certificates and zero job offers. We did not sit down and say, let us build a model cooperative to inspire the nation. We said, we have to eat, so what can we do together that we cannot do separately? Looking back, the desperation was actually the right starting point. We had nothing to protect, so we were not afraid to try things. We made our first real money from a church grille order that we quoted for because none of us thought we'd get it. Then we got it, and we had to work three weekends in a row to deliver it on time. That's how you build confidence — not by thinking about it, but by doing it under pressure.
What was the hardest part of the first year?
Trust. Not trust from the outside world — we expected them to doubt us. The hardest thing was trust among ourselves. Twelve women who had been through the same NAP cohort — we thought we knew each other. Then you put money and work and reputation on the same table and you find out who keeps their word. We lost one member in the first six months over a pricing dispute. We nearly lost two more over how to split a large contract. We had to write rules for things we hadn't thought about because we'd never run a business before. The COTVET field officer who visited us in month four — she was the one who told us to write everything down. That was the best advice we received. Write everything down before there is a dispute, not after.
What would you say to women considering a cooperative model versus trying to build individually?
I would say: the cooperative is not for everyone, and it requires you to share things that are uncomfortable to share — money, credit, the work itself. But for women entering male-dominated trades without networks, without capital, without anyone who looks like them to introduce them to clients — the cooperative solves all of those problems at once. I had eleven people I could call when a big order came in. I had eleven people whose networks connected to mine. When a client tried to negotiate our rate down because we were women, there were twelve of us to say no — and twelve of us to walk away if he didn't respect it. You can say no much more easily when you're not alone.

Unsafe by Design: Workplace Standards and the Gender Gap
Ghana's Factories, Offices and Shops Act was last substantively amended in 2003. Its provisions on workplace health and safety were written for male workers in male workplaces. Women entering the trades are navigating legal and physical environments designed without them.
By Adjoa Asante-Twum, TVET & Skills Policy Correspondent

When Abena Mensah joined her first plumbing crew in Takoradi's energy corridor, she found that the standard-issue PPE — gloves, overalls, safety boots — came in one size range. The boots were a minimum size 8. Her feet are a size 5. The overalls were cut for a male torso. "I wore them rolled up and belted for three months until I could afford to buy my own," she says. "It wasn't dangerous — it was just a daily reminder that no one had imagined me in this role."

The PPE problem is symptomatic of a wider issue: Ghana's construction and trades workplace safety standards are built on an assumed male worker. The Labour Act 2003 and the Factories, Offices and Shops Act 1970 (partially updated 2003) set minimum standards for workplace facilities that include no gender-disaggregated provisions for sanitary facilities, changing rooms, or rest areas. Employers are not legally required to provide female-specific facilities for workers in any trade category, and most do not.

Harassment and the Unreported Reality

Our survey of 62 women in skilled trades included questions about workplace harassment. The results were sobering: 74% reported experiencing verbal harassment related to gender at some point in their trade career; 42% reported it occurring regularly in their current work environment; and 28% reported an incident that constituted sexual harassment under the Labour Act definition. Of that 28%, fewer than 10% had made any formal complaint — the remainder citing fear of job loss, belief that nothing would change, or absence of any reporting mechanism.

The Labour Commission handles workplace harassment complaints, but its capacity in Ghana's regions outside Accra is limited. In the Northern Region, there is one Labour Commission officer covering four districts. The practical reality is that women in skilled trades who experience harassment have no effective formal recourse and know it. This is not a cultural inevitability — it is a resource allocation failure.

Physical Environment: The Training Centre Problem

TVET training centres present a specific challenge. Female students in trades programmes report — consistently across all six regions visited — that the physical environment of training workshops was not designed for them. This includes: workshop changing and toilet facilities shared with male students (or, in several cases, no female toilet facilities at all); training tools and equipment scaled for male hand sizes; protective equipment not available in female sizes; and workshop cultures where female students are expected to observe before participating, on timelines that exceed those applied to male students.

The TVET Service has a gender-inclusive design standard — published in 2022 — that sets minimum facility requirements for institutions seeking or renewing programme accreditation. In practice, compliance is uneven. Of the 14 TVET institutions visited in our field research, six had no dedicated female changing or sanitary facilities in their technical workshops. Three of those six held current accreditation from the TVET Service.
