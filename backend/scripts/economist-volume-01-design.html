A Nation of Builders Waiting to Be Built
Letter from the Editor-in-Chief

Ghana is not short of talent. Walk through the workshops of Suame Magazine in Kumasi — the largest informal industrial cluster on the African continent — and you will encounter mechanics who can diagnose a diesel engine by ear, welders whose joints could hold a bridge together, and carpenters whose work graces the finest homes in Cantonments. The hands that built this country are extraordinary.

And yet something is broken. Ghana's unemployment rate among those aged 15 to 35 hovers around 12 percent — but the youth underemployment figure, capturing those working far below their potential, stretches to nearly 40 percent. Across the country, employers report critical skills shortages in fields from construction to healthcare, from software engineering to electrical installation. Supply and demand have become tragically misaligned.

This inaugural edition of the LocalLink Economist sets out to understand why. Over the past three months, our team has travelled to Accra, Kumasi, Tamale, Takoradi, and Cape Coast. We have spoken to 200 workers, 60 employers, 15 policymakers, and a dozen academics. The picture that emerges is complex, human, and urgent.

There is genuine cause for optimism. Ghana's TVET sector, long stigmatised as the destination of those who failed the university entrance exam, is experiencing a quiet renaissance. The National Apprenticeship Programme has registered over 300,000 apprentices since 2022. Women are entering skilled trades in rising numbers. The digital economy is creating entirely new categories of skilled work that did not exist a decade ago.

But the obstacles are formidable. Brain drain continues to bleed the country of its most expensively trained professionals. Wage inequality between Accra and the north is widening, not narrowing. And the informal economy — lauded by some as proof of Ghanaian entrepreneurialism — too often means exploited workers with no protections and no path to advancement.

We launch this magazine because we believe the conversation about Ghana's labour market deserves more than policy briefs and development-bank reports. It deserves rigour, narrative, and honesty. We hope this first volume earns your trust and your challenge.

Kwame A. Mensah
Editor-in-Chief, LocalLink Economist

A Nation of Hands:
Ghana's Skilled Labour
Economy in 2026
Photo: Nana Addo Boateng / LocalLink Economist
Ghana has 13 million working-age adults in the formal and informal economy. Fewer than a third have recognised qualifications. The rest — the majority — work by instinct, by apprenticeship, and by necessity. Understanding them is the only way to build an economy that works.
By Kofi Darko Asante  ·  Senior Correspondent, Labour & Industry

The morning shift at the Tema Industrial Area begins before sunrise. By 5:45am, hundreds of workers in overalls and hard hats are threading through the gates of factories, workshops, and depots. Some have travelled two hours from Ashaiman; others live in compounds that crowd the estate itself. All of them are, in some sense, skilled: they operate lathes, read blueprints, mix chemicals. Most have never held a certificate that says so.

Ghana's skilled labour market is, above all else, enormous and informal. Of approximately 13 million people in the active workforce, the Ghana Statistical Service estimates that just 31 percent hold any nationally recognised qualification. The remaining 69 percent have acquired their skills through traditional apprenticeships, on-the-job learning, or family transmission going back generations.

"We have been training artisans in this country for 400 years. We just forgot to give them certificates for it."
— Ing. Kwabena Asante-Poku, Ghana Institution of Engineers

This is not unusual in sub-Saharan Africa. But Ghana's particular challenge is that the gap between informal competence and formal recognition is growing wider at exactly the moment when global supply chains demand traceable, certifiable workforces. A Ghanaian contractor bidding for a World Bank project must provide certified tradespeople. The informal skills that built the country are, quietly, becoming commercially insufficient.

Nowhere is this tension more visible than in construction — Ghana's second-largest employer of skilled labour after agriculture. The sector employs an estimated 800,000 people: masons, carpenters, plumbers, electricians, steel fixers — the overwhelming majority of whom learned their trade from a master craftsman in an arrangement unchanged since the colonial era.

"I have been building houses for twenty-two years," says Kwesi Agyei, 46, foreman in Spintex. "I have built over 200 structures. But if I apply for a senior site manager job on a formal project, they ask for my NVQ certificate. I do not have one. So I am invisible to them."

The Sector Breakdown

Ghana's formal skilled labour market is dominated by four sectors. Construction and real estate accounts for roughly 19 percent of skilled employment. The broader services sector — encompassing banking, telecoms, retail, hospitality, and the rapidly expanding tech industry — employs around 28 percent. Manufacturing contributes 16 percent. Healthcare employs approximately 8 percent of formally recognised skilled workers.

Agriculture, which remains Ghana's largest sector by workforce share (41 percent of total employment), presents a paradox. Cocoa farming requires extraordinary skill: grafting, pest management, fermentation, post-harvest handling. But agricultural workers are almost entirely classified as "unskilled" in national statistics — a categorisation that enrages rural economists and reflects a deep urban bias in how Ghana defines and values labour.

"If you can manage a 10-hectare cocoa farm, negotiate with LBC buyers, maintain soil health, and predict yield — that is not unskilled work," says Dr. Edith Dankwa of GIMPA. "We have made a category error that distorts our entire labour policy."

The Wage Ladder

Among formally employed skilled workers, wages vary dramatically. Software engineers in Accra's tech ecosystem command GH₵ 8,000–15,000 per month. Junior doctors and nurses in public hospitals earn GH₵ 4,000–7,000. A certified electrician working for a construction firm earns perhaps GH₵ 1,800–2,500 monthly. A plumber on daily rates might clear GH₵ 80–120 per day when work is available — which is rarely five days a week, year-round.

The regional disparity is stark. The median monthly wage for a skilled urban worker in Greater Accra is GH₵ 2,780. In the Northern Region, the figure falls to GH₵ 1,340 — barely half. In the Upper East and Upper West regions, the median drops further still. The North is not lacking in skilled workers; it is lacking in employers willing to pay skilled wages.

"The North is not lacking in skilled workers — it is lacking in employers willing to pay skilled wages."
— Ama Boateng, Data Journalist, LocalLink Economist

This geographic wage gap has a predictable consequence: southward migration. The Ghana Living Standards Survey of 2024 found that 38 percent of Northern Region youth aged 18–30 had relocated or planned to relocate to Greater Accra, Ashanti, or Western regions within three years. The towns and villages of the north are aging. The skilled young are leaving.

Feature
The Departure Tax: How Ghana Subsidises the World's Workforce
Every year, Ghana spends approximately GH₵ 1.8 billion training doctors, engineers, and nurses. Thousands of them never work a single day in Ghana. This is the story of that extraordinary subsidy — and who pays for it.
By Abena Osei-Kuffour  ·  Deputy Editor, Economics

The University of Ghana Medical School graduates between 250 and 300 doctors every year. In 2024, an analysis by the Ghana Health Service found that just 41 percent of medical graduates in the five-year cohort 2019–2023 were still working in Ghana. The rest had emigrated — primarily to the United Kingdom, Canada, and the United States.

The pattern repeats across professions. The Nurses and Midwives Council estimates that 18,000 registered nurses left the country between 2020 and 2025, many recruited directly by NHS England. The Ghana Institution of Engineers puts the emigration rate for newly licensed engineers at approximately 22 percent within three years of qualification.

"I do not think of it as leaving," says Abena Frimpong, 28, a software engineer who moved to Berlin in 2023. "I think of it as extending my education. There are things I learn here that I can bring back. Maybe. The question is when 'maybe' becomes 'never.'"

"41% of medical graduates from Ghana's 2019–2023 cohort were still working in Ghana. The rest had emigrated."
— Ghana Health Service Analysis, 2024

The economic arithmetic is brutal. Training a doctor in Ghana costs the state approximately GH₵ 180,000–220,000. When that doctor emigrates and builds a career in the NHS, Ghana has effectively made a gift to the British health system. The World Bank estimated in 2023 that sub-Saharan African countries collectively transfer more in trained human capital to OECD nations each year than they receive in official development aid.

The irony is that many Ghanaian emigrants remain emotionally connected to home. Remittances totalled $4.7 billion in 2024, making them Ghana's second-largest source of foreign exchange after gold. The brain drain and the remittance economy are two sides of the same coin.

Feature
The School Nobody Wanted to Attend — Until Now
Ghana's TVET sector has long been the education system's unloved stepchild. A new generation of graduates, employers, and reformers are changing that narrative — slowly, but with gathering momentum.
By Yaw Owusu  ·  Features Writer

In the Ghanaian school system, the phrase "you will end up in a technical school" was, for generations, a threat. When a child failed the Basic Education Certificate Examination, the path to a vocational institution was presented not as an alternative but as a consolation — evidence of academic inadequacy. The stigma was reinforced by families, teachers, and employers alike, and produced predictable results: TVET institutions chronically underfunded, undersubscribed, and equipped with machinery dating in some cases to the 1970s.

That story is changing, though not yet fast enough. Between 2020 and 2025, enrolment in accredited TVET programmes increased by 34 percent. The National Apprenticeship Programme has registered over 300,000 apprentices. Several private technical training providers — among them Cowtec Institute and the Zongo Skills Centre in Kumasi — have built strong employer partnerships and impressive graduate employment rates.

"When I told my father I was going to Accra Technical University, he was disappointed," says Samuel Asante, 24, now a certified electrical engineer earning GH₵ 3,200 monthly in the oil and gas sector. "Two years later, he was asking his friends to send their children there. That is the shift."

But the structural challenges are formidable. COTVET operates with a budget that remains a fraction of the Ministry of Education's university allocation. Equipment upgrades lag years behind industry requirements: electrical training workshops teaching students on panel boards that predate digital control systems; automotive labs servicing carburettor engines in an era of ECU-managed fuel injection.

"How do I train a student to service a modern EV when my workshop's newest vehicle is a 2003 Toyota Corolla?"
— Head of Dept., Automotive Technology, Kumasi Technical University

Curriculum reform has also lagged. The TVET curriculum was last comprehensively overhauled in 2015. Since then, the construction industry has adopted Building Information Modelling; manufacturing has embraced Industry 4.0 automation; the energy sector has pivoted toward solar. None of these are adequately represented in current TVET syllabuses.

There are green shoots. The TVET Policy 2022 committed the government to a 70:30 STEM ratio in vocational curricula. The Ghana Employers' Association has piloted a "dual apprenticeship" model adapted from Germany. Early results are promising. But pilots are not policy, and good intentions are not budgets.

Feature
The Women Rewiring Ghana's Trades
Female electricians, welders, quantity surveyors, and mechanics are entering Ghana's skilled trades at unprecedented rates — challenging gender norms and stale assumptions about who builds the country.
By Efua Quartey  ·  Policy Correspondent

Grace Adwoa Sarpong, 27, has been a licensed electrician for three years. Her tools are organised with the meticulousness of a surgeon's tray; her knowledge of the National Wiring Regulations is encyclopaedic; her client list, built entirely by word of mouth in East Legon, is longer than she can service. She also happens to be, in many of her clients' homes, the first female electrician they have ever employed.

"At first they always want to call my supervisor to confirm I know what I am doing," she says, laughing. "Then I fix the thing. Then they refer me to five of their neighbours." The pattern — initial scepticism followed by professional conversion — is one Grace's fellow female tradespeople recognise uniformly.

Women account for an estimated 18 percent of graduates from TVET programmes in technical disciplines. That share has nearly doubled since 2018, driven by targeted scholarship programmes and female-only cohorts at institutions like Kumasi Technical University.

The economics of gender in skilled trades are complex. Women in the formal sector — those employed by construction firms or utilities — generally earn the same as male counterparts with equivalent qualifications. The National Labour Commission's 2024 audit found no statistically significant gender pay gap in formally employed technical workers.

"My male colleagues charge GH₵ 150 per day. I charged GH₵ 100. Now I charge GH₵ 180. It took three years to get there."
— Female electrician, Tema, age 31

The informal sector is a different story. Self-employed female tradespeople report consistently charging lower rates than male equivalents, partly by customer expectation and partly by their own initial reluctance to assert market value. The gap narrows sharply with experience and reputation. But it is real, and it matters.

What the data cannot capture is the symbolic significance of the shift. When a girl in Tamale sees a female engineer on a construction hoarding; when a TVET counsellor says "we have many successful women in this programme" — something structural is changing. It changes slowly. But it is changing.

Regional Focus
Suame Magazine: Africa's Greatest Workshop — and Its Greatest Untapped Asset
200,000 workers. 12,000 workshops. Generations of inherited craft knowledge. Suame Magazine is Ghana's most extraordinary labour market. It is also almost entirely invisible to the formal economy.
By Kofi Darko Asante  ·  Senior Correspondent

To walk through Suame Magazine is to understand that the dichotomy between "formal" and "informal" labour is a bureaucratic convenience, not an economic reality. Here, in a sprawling 400-acre complex of sheds and workshops in the heart of Kumasi, 200,000 workers practise trades of extraordinary complexity. Men in their twenties diagnose computer-managed engine faults with diagnostic tablets. Fabricators build custom trailers for mining companies. Foundry workers cast aluminium components sold across West Africa.

The cluster was established in the 1970s, when Kumasi's mechanics began congregating in this district for mutual convenience. It has grown organically ever since, now employing more workers than Ghana's entire formal manufacturing sector. The average worker has been in their trade for 14 years. The average master craftsman has trained between 8 and 12 apprentices.

Yet Suame Magazine is almost entirely excluded from Ghana's formal economy. Workers are not registered with SSNIT. They do not hold recognised qualifications. They cannot bid on government contracts. They cannot access commercial loans — at least not from banks, who view their income as unverifiable. They exist in an economic twilight that is simultaneously extraordinarily productive and utterly precarious.

"I have trained eleven apprentices in thirty years. Not one of them has a certificate. But every one of them can fix anything you bring me."
— Master mechanic, Suame Magazine, age 58

"When the government comes, they come to take," says Emmanuel Acheampong, 41, an auto electrician. "They have not yet come to give." Until the social contract changes, formalisation will remain aspirational — and Suame's extraordinary workers will remain invisible on the ledgers that measure Ghana's prosperity.

Policy Analysis
The National Apprenticeship Programme: Promise vs Performance
Ghana's flagship skills programme has 300,000 registered apprentices and strong political backing. But implementation gaps, funding shortfalls, and a persistent quality problem are limiting its transformative potential.
By Efua Quartey  ·  Policy Correspondent

The National Apprenticeship Programme (NAP), launched by the Ministry of Employment in 2022, aimed to bring Ghana's 2.1 million informal apprentices into a formal framework: registered, assessed, certified, and connected to employers who would provide placement opportunities upon graduation. Three years in, the headline numbers are impressive. 312,000 apprentices are registered. 68,000 have completed the programme and received certificates. COTVET reports that 74 percent of certified graduates found employment within six months.

But the picture below the headlines is more complicated. Fieldwork by LocalLink Economist, conducted across 12 districts between September and November 2025, found persistent problems with programme quality. In several districts, NAP was little more than a registration exercise: apprentices were enrolled on paper, their masters collected stipend payments of GH₵ 400 per month per apprentice, and no substantive assessment or training support was provided. "I signed the form," says one master baker in Koforidua. "I received the money. No one came to assess me or my apprentices. What programme?"

The Assessment Problem

The assessment system is a particular weakness. COTVET has 340 certified assessors for over 300,000 registered apprentices — a ratio that makes meaningful individual assessment logistically impossible. The result is "certificate inflation": the credential exists but carries limited information about actual competency. Employers in the formal sector are beginning to notice, with several construction firms reverting to their own in-house testing.

The programme's champions argue that these are scaling problems, not design failures. "Any programme of this size will have implementation gaps in its early years," says a senior official at the Ministry, speaking off the record. "The infrastructure is being built at the same time as the programme is running. That is not ideal. But the alternative was to wait until everything was perfect, and never start."

"Better to certify 50,000 workers properly than 300,000 workers on paper."
— Prof. Kojo E. Aryeetey, ISSER/UG

Critics, led by researchers at ISSER, argue that the political incentive to report large enrolment numbers is precisely the problem. A programme designed to demonstrate scale will always prioritise quantity over quality. Real reform requires binding minimum standards for assessment frequency, mandatory employer engagement, and an independent verification mechanism — none of which currently exist within the NAP framework.

In Conversation
Hon. Cecilia
Dapaah-Ntim
Minister of Employment and Labour Relations, Republic of Ghana
Hon. Cecilia Dapaah-Ntim
Interviewed at the Ministry of Employment, Accra, December 2025. Interview edited for length and clarity.
Interviewed by Abena Osei-Kuffour, Deputy Editor
Minister, the NAP has 300,000 registered apprentices. But critics say the quality is inconsistent. How do you respond?
Every large programme has teething problems. What I will tell you is that the 68,000 young people who have completed the NAP and hold certificates are in far better positions than they would have been without it. We acknowledge the quality challenges. That is why we are tripling the number of assessors in 2026 and introducing digital assessment tools that allow remote evaluation. We are building the aircraft while flying it, yes — but the alternative was to leave 300,000 young Ghanaians with no recognised pathway at all.
Ghana loses thousands of nurses and doctors to the UK every year. Does the government have a real strategy to address this?
This is one of the most difficult challenges we face, and I will not pretend otherwise. The salary differential is real. A newly qualified nurse in the NHS earns the sterling equivalent of approximately GH₵ 18,000 per month. We cannot match that. What we can do — and are doing — is improve working conditions, accelerate specialist training, and negotiate ethical recruitment agreements with destination countries. The UK-Ghana health worker compact of 2024 was a step. More steps are needed.
What is your 2030 vision for Ghana's skills landscape?
By 2030, I want every Ghanaian with a marketable skill to have a nationally recognised credential for it — whether acquired in a university, a TVET institution, an apprenticeship, or forty years on the job. I want regional wage gaps to be narrowing, not widening. And I want the young woman in Tamale who wants to be an engineer to see our TVET system as a genuine ladder to that ambition, not a consolation prize. That is the vision. We are nowhere near it yet. But it is the right direction.

Stop Romanticising the Informal Economy
The informal sector is not a triumph of Ghanaian entrepreneurialism. It is a symptom of policy failure. Treating it as anything else is dangerous self-congratulation.
Prof. Kojo Ennin Aryeetey  ·  Institute of Statistical, Social & Economic Research, University of Ghana, Legon

There is a rhetorical move that Ghanaian politicians, development economists, and well-meaning journalists make with depressing regularity. Confronted with data showing that 68 percent of Ghana's workforce is informally employed, they pivot to celebration: look at the ingenuity of our market women, the resilience of our artisans, the entrepreneurial spirit of our informal economy. It is emotionally satisfying, culturally resonant, and analytically bankrupt.

Let us be clear about what the informal economy actually is. It is the portion of economic activity that occurs outside the framework of legal registration, tax obligation, labour protection, and social insurance. Informal workers cannot access SSNIT pensions. They have no sick leave, no maternity pay, no recourse when injured. They cannot access formal credit. They cannot bid for government contracts. They are, in the most material sense, economically second-class citizens — and we applaud them for their "resilience" in tolerating these conditions.

The Suame Magazine story is instructive. The 200,000 workers in that complex are genuinely skilled. Their productivity is real. Their contribution to the Kumasi economy is substantial. But they operate without the institutional scaffolding that would allow them to grow, invest, protect themselves, and pass wealth to their children. An informal mechanic who is just as skilled as a formally employed engineer earns a fraction as much, has no path to promotion, no pension, and no ability to borrow against his future earnings. That is not resilience. That is deprivation with a positive reframe.

The danger of romanticisation is not merely rhetorical. It shapes policy. When government officials point to the informal sector's dynamism as evidence that things are fundamentally fine, they reduce the urgency of the reforms that would actually integrate these workers into formal economic life: the SSNIT reform, the SME credit guarantee scheme, the Recognition of Prior Learning framework that would allow informal workers to gain credentials for skills they already have.

"We applaud informal workers for their 'resilience' in tolerating conditions that no formal worker would accept. That is not a compliment. It is a failing."
— Prof. Kojo E. Aryeetey

I am not suggesting that the informal sector should be criminalised or that its workers are victims without agency. Many informal entrepreneurs are building real businesses; many artisans prefer flexibility to formal employment. The point is that the choice between formal and informal should be a genuine choice, made from a position of equal information and opportunity. Currently, it is not.

Ghana's skilled labour crisis will not be resolved until we stop treating the informal economy as an achievement and start treating it as the challenge it is. Formalisation is not an imposition on workers. Properly designed, it is an expansion of their rights, their options, and their dignity. That should be the goal. And it should be stated plainly, without the defensive celebration that currently obscures it.

Looking Ahead
76
Five Priorities for Ghana's Labour Market in 2026
The editors of LocalLink Economist identify the most consequential policy and investment priorities for the year ahead
1. Fix the NAP's Quality Problem Before It Becomes a Credibility Crisis

The National Apprenticeship Programme's biggest asset is political momentum. Its biggest liability is the gap between its credential and actual competency. If employers stop trusting the certificate — as several are beginning to — the programme's value collapses. The government must invest urgently in assessor capacity: the target of 1,500 additional certified assessors by end-2026 is achievable but only with a specific budget allocation. Digital assessment tools can bridge the gap while physical capacity is built.

2. Formalise Ghana's Informal Industrial Clusters

Suame Magazine and dozens of smaller clusters represent billions of cedis in productive capacity and hundreds of thousands of skilled workers who are invisible to the formal economy. A Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL) framework — national, standardised, and actively resourced — would allow these workers to gain credentials for skills they already have, without returning to school. Ghana has had a draft RPL policy "under review" since 2020. It needs to be enacted, funded, and implemented.

3. Address Brain Drain Systematically, Not Rhetorically

The health worker emigration crisis cannot be solved by closing borders or moral suasion. It requires a genuine salary adjustment in the public health sector targeted at the highest-attrition roles, combined with investment in working conditions and specialist training pathways that make Ghana competitive on dimensions other than pure salary. A public-private partnership model, where private hospitals co-fund public-sector specialist training in exchange for assured staffing pools, deserves serious exploration.

4. Close the Regional Wage and Opportunity Gap

The GH₵ 1,440 monthly wage gap between Greater Accra and the Upper East region is not solely a labour market phenomenon — it reflects a profound inequality in economic infrastructure and state investment. The Tamale Special Economic Zone, if properly resourced and governed, could be transformative; its current status as a planning document is not. Targeted infrastructure investment in the North and deliberate incentives for employers to locate outside the south deserve urgent attention.

5. Reform TVET Funding and Governance

Ghana's TVET sector cannot transform itself on its current budget allocation, which represents approximately 4.2 percent of the education budget despite enrolling over 450,000 students. The immediate priorities are equipment upgrades (a GH₵ 500 million fund structured as matching grants between institutions and industry partners), curriculum reform to incorporate Industry 4.0 content, and mandatory industry advisory boards at every accredited TVET institution. The TVET Levy allocation to training institutions should be doubled from 30 to 60 percent of collections.

"Ghana does not have a talent shortage. It has a talent allocation problem, a certification problem, and a wages problem. All three are solvable."
— The Editors, LocalLink Economist
