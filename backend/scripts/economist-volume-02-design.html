From the Editor
05
The Price of Building Ghana
Letter from the Editor-in-Chief

In January 2025, a 50kg bag of ordinary Portland cement cost GH₵ 75 at a Tema hardware store. By December 2025, the same bag cost GH₵ 90 — a 20 percent increase in twelve months. On its own, that is a number. Multiplied across a medium-density residential project of 2,000 square metres consuming perhaps 2,500 bags, it becomes GH₵ 37,500 of additional cost on a single line item. Add the corresponding increases in reinforcement steel, aluminium roofing, copper wiring, and skilled labour rates, and you begin to understand why housing developers in Accra are shelving projects, why school contractors are requesting variation orders, and why the ordinary Ghanaian who wants to build a three-bedroom house is finding the dream receding faster than they can save.

This second edition of LocalLink Economist is our response to what we believe is one of the most consequential but least understood dynamics in Ghana's economy: the cost of construction. We have spent four months compiling 142 price data points across nine regions, surveying suppliers, contractors, and government procurement databases, and building what we believe to be Ghana's most comprehensive independent construction pricing index.

What we found should alarm policymakers, builders, and prospective homeowners in equal measure. The LocalLink CIPI (Construction and Infrastructure Pricing Index) stood at 128.4 at the end of Q4 2025 — up 8.3 percent year-on-year and fully 28.4 percent above our base year of Q1 2023. Structural materials — cement, steel, and precast concrete — are the primary drivers. But the deepest and most troubling story is in the data on regional price variation. A contractor building a government school in Tamale faces total material costs approximately 34 percent higher than an equivalent project in Accra. That premium is not abstract: it represents government money wasted, infrastructure not built, and communities not served.

We have also included, for the first time, a full construction labour rate survey covering 24 trades across all nine regions. The pattern is consistent with what we found in our inaugural labour market edition: skilled workers in the north are paid less, but the materials they build with cost more. It is a paradox that has deep consequences for national infrastructure equity.

We present this data not as a counsel of despair but as a foundation for better decisions — by government procurers, private developers, quantity surveyors, and the millions of Ghanaians navigating one of the most expensive decisions of their lives: whether, and how, to build.

Kwame A. Mensah
Editor-in-Chief, LocalLink Economist

The LocalLink Construction and Infrastructure Pricing Index is compiled quarterly from surveys of registered suppliers, import records at Tema and Takoradi ports, and government procurement databases across nine administrative regions. Each of the 142 price data points is weighted by its typical share of total project cost for a standardised medium-density residential building — our reference project — with supplementary indices for road construction, institutional buildings (schools and clinics), and water infrastructure.

The Q4 2025 reading of 128.4 represents the highest level since the index was initiated in Q1 2023. The acceleration in Q3 and Q4 2025 — adding 6.2 points in the second half of the year alone — reflects the compounding effects of cedi depreciation on imported material costs, the global steel price recovery, and what suppliers describe as supply chain disruptions in the local cement market. We examine each of these drivers in detail in the pages that follow.

Tamale
Northern Region  ·  +34% premium
The highest construction cost premium in the country. Materials must travel 600–800km from Tema port or Accra warehouses. Road freight cost for a truckload of cement: GH₵ 2,800–3,400. Local competition among suppliers is limited. Government procurement records show per-m² costs for public buildings in Tamale averaging GH₵ 3,200 vs GH₵ 2,390 in Accra — a 34% gap that directly reduces the number of schools, clinics, and roads the public infrastructure budget can deliver.

In Conversation
Arch. Patrick
Acheampong-Kusi
President, Ghana Real Estate Developers Association (GREDA)
Interviewed by Kofi Darko Asante, Senior Correspondent
The CIPI is up 8.3% year-on-year. What does that mean for Ghana's housing developers?
It means we are building fewer houses than we should be. When cement goes from GH₵ 75 to GH₵ 90 in twelve months, and steel goes up 18%, and copper wiring 24%, a developer who priced a project in early 2025 is now facing a bill 15–20% higher than their BoQ. Most developers are not in a position to absorb that. Projects are being shelved. Contracts are being renegotiated. Some are simply being abandoned. At a time when Ghana has a housing deficit of approximately 1.8 million units, we cannot afford this.
Is the cement price increase a market failure, or just the cost of doing business in a global commodity environment?
It is both — but the domestic element is the more troubling one. GPCL and CIMAF together control the overwhelming majority of cement production capacity. When they announce a price increase, there is no effective competition to hold the price down. We have called for the government to open cement importation more freely and remove the tariff protection that keeps domestic prices above the cost that competitive imports would achieve. The government has not moved on this. Until it does, cement prices will reflect the interests of producers, not the interests of the housing market or the public infrastructure budget.
What policy changes would most help the real estate and construction sector in 2026?
Three things. First, tariff review on construction materials — particularly cement, steel, and aluminium. Second, the GH₵ 2 billion affordable housing fund in the budget needs to be disbursed, not recycled as an announcement. Third, the government must reform the Rent Control Act to make the private rental market more investable. If developers cannot price risk appropriately in the rental market, they will not build rental housing, and the deficit will widen further. These are not radical demands. They are the baseline conditions for a functional construction sector.

The Tamale School That Never Got Built: How Construction Cost Inequality Fails Ghana's Public Budget
Every percentage point of regional price premium represents classrooms not built, boreholes not drilled, and clinics not opened. We have been calculating this wrong for decades.
Ing. Kwabena Asante-Poku  ·  Past President, Ghana Institution of Engineers (GhIE)

Ghana's national infrastructure budget is not allocated on the basis of what things actually cost in different parts of the country. It is allocated on the basis of population, political geography, and line ministry estimates that use Accra prices with, at best, a notional transport cost uplift. The result is a systematic and persistent underfunding of infrastructure in precisely the regions that need it most: the north, the Volta Region, the Upper Regions.

Consider the arithmetic of a 3-classroom block. In Accra, GH₵ 820,000 builds one. In Tamale, the same specification costs GH₵ 1,098,800. If the Ministry of Education allocates GH₵ 8.2 million for ten classroom blocks "in the north" — using Accra unit rates — it will actually deliver seven, not ten. The three missing classrooms are invisible in the budget. They appear as contractor "variation orders" or, more frequently, as incomplete projects where the money ran out.

I have spent thirty years reviewing public sector construction contracts in Ghana. The pattern is consistent and maddening. A district assembly receives a budget based on national average unit rates. The Tamale DA goes to tender. Every competent bidder — knowing the true cost of transport, labour mobilisation, and materials in the north — prices above the budget. The DA either accepts the cheapest bidder who intends to cut corners, or the project is not awarded at all. Neither outcome serves the people of Tamale.

The solution is not complicated. The National Development Planning Commission and the Ministry of Finance need to develop and maintain a Regional Infrastructure Cost Adjustment Table — published annually, tied to actual market data, and used mandatorily in all public sector budget estimation. This is what the UK's HM Treasury does with its Infrastructure and Projects Authority construction cost data. It is what the South African National Treasury does with its ASAQS benchmarks. Ghana has the data. What it has lacked is the institutional commitment to use it.

"Ghana allocates infrastructure budgets using Accra prices. The north pays the difference in classrooms not built and boreholes not drilled."
— Ing. Kwabena Asante-Poku

There is also a procurement reform dimension. The Ghana PPA Act requires awarding to the lowest evaluated bidder. In a market with real regional cost differentials, this creates a race to the bottom in which the winning bidder has priced impossibly low and will deliver impossibly little. The PPA should be amended to allow reserve prices calibrated to regional benchmarks — prices below which a bid is presumed non-compliant with specification, not merely competitive.

These reforms would cost nothing except the political will to implement them. The alternative — continuing to build infrastructure in the north at prices that assume it is located in Accra — is a subsidy that flows not to the people of Tamale, but to the contractors who deliver less than promised and the officials who sign off on it.

The Tema Port Bottleneck

Ghana's construction sector is critically dependent on Tema Port — the country's main deep-water port and entry point for an estimated 68% of all imported construction materials. In 2025, average dwell time for construction-related imports at Tema was 11.4 days, down from 14.2 days in 2023 but still substantially above the port's own target of 7 days and the West African benchmark of 5.8 days (Abidjan) or 4.1 days (Dakar). The cost of those extra days is not trivial. For a 20-foot container, each additional day at port costs GH₵ 180–380 in demurrage and storage. On a modest project importing 15 containers, the difference between a 7-day and 11-day dwell time represents GH₵ 16,000–23,000 in entirely avoidable cost.

The Forex Transmission Problem

Ghana imports approximately 62% of its construction materials by value. Every exchange rate movement transmits directly into construction costs. When the cedi depreciated 6.2% against the USD in 2025, importers of copper cable, sanitary ware, ceramic tiles, and MEP components faced automatic cost increases on their next shipment. There is no hedging mechanism available to most Ghanaian construction material importers. They absorb the forex risk entirely — and pass it on entirely.

Mrs. Abena
Sarpong-Adu
President, Ghana Institution of Surveyors (GhIS) & Director, Apex Quantity Surveyors Ltd
On cost management, BoQ reform, and the QS profession in Ghana — Interviewed by Kwesi Antwi-Boasiako
Contractors are reporting average overruns of 10–22% in 2025. Is this a failure of quantity surveying or of the contract framework?
Both, but primarily the contract framework. A competent quantity surveyor will price a BoQ accurately at a point in time — but a BoQ is not a crystal ball. When cement moves 20% in nine months, any fixed-price BoQ becomes inaccurate within weeks of signing. The profession is not at fault for market movements we cannot predict. What we are responsible for is advising clients on the appropriate contract mechanism — and that means insisting on escalation clauses in contracts of more than six months' duration. Too many QS firms in Ghana accept fixed-price contracts uncritically because the client wants cost certainty. Cost certainty achieved through an impossible contract is not certainty — it is a deferred dispute.
You have been pushing for reform of the standard government BoQ format. What specifically needs to change?
Several things. First, the government's standard BoQ format still does not separate materials from labour in most bill items — which makes it impossible to apply materials escalation formulae accurately. You need unit rates broken into their components if you want to adjust them properly. Second, provisional sums and contingency allowances in government BoQs are set at 2–3% of project cost as a matter of formula — regardless of project risk profile. In the current environment, a realistic contingency for a 12-month materials-intensive project is 12–15%. Third, there is no standard mechanism for getting a variation order processed in less than 60 days on a government project. By the time the variation is approved, the contractor has already absorbed the loss. Contractors price the anticipated loss into their next tender. The system breeds distrust and inflated bids.
What is your single most important message for developers and public sector clients in 2026?
Stop treating the quantity surveyor as a form-filler who produces the BoQ after all design decisions have been made. Bring your QS in at feasibility stage, not tender stage. We can tell you whether a project is affordable before you spend GH₵ 400,000 on full design. We can identify value engineering opportunities while the design is still fluid. And we can structure the contract correctly from the start — not scramble to fix it when the contractor is 30% over budget and threatening to walk off site. The profession has the tools. We need to be given the authority to use them.

CIPI Methodology & Data Sources
98
How We Build the CIPI — Full Methodology Disclosure
Technical disclosure of data collection, weighting, index calculation, and quality assurance for the LocalLink Construction & Infrastructure Pricing Index. Q4 2025.
Data Collection

The CIPI is compiled from three primary data streams. First, direct price surveys: our team of 12 field researchers visits registered construction materials suppliers in each of Ghana's nine administrative regions quarterly, collecting current retail prices for each of the 142 line items in the index basket. Suppliers are drawn from our standing panel of 180 firms, stratified by region and product category, representing a mix of major distributors, regional wholesalers, and independent retailers. A minimum of three price observations per line item per region are collected; outliers (more than 1.5 standard deviations from the regional mean) are investigated and either corrected or excluded.

The second data stream is government procurement records. The Public Procurement Authority (PPA) provides, with a 45-day lag, tender price data from completed award notices for public sector construction contracts above threshold. This data allows validation of field survey prices against actual procurement outcomes and identification of systematic divergences between retail and government procurement pricing.

The third stream is import data from the Ghana Customs and Excise Division. For materials with significant import content (steel, MEP components, tiles, sanitary ware), import unit values declared at Tema and Takoradi ports provide an independent check on retail price movements and allow early identification of imported-cost pressure before it fully transmits to retail prices.

Index Weighting

The CIPI is a Laspeyres price index with a fixed quantity basket updated every three years. The basket reflects the cost structure of our "Reference Project" — a standardised 500m² GFA, two-storey, reinforced concrete frame residential building designed in consultation with the Ghana Institution of Surveyors. Major category weights: Structural Materials 30.2%; MEP and Services 18.4%; Finishes and Cladding 16.8%; Skilled Labour 13.2%; Earthworks and Drainage 8.6%; Plant and Preliminaries 7.4%; Roofing and Waterproofing 5.4%.

Regional Index Calculation

The national CIPI is a GDP-weighted average of nine regional indices reflecting each region's approximate share of Ghana's total construction output. Regional weighting: Greater Accra 42%; Ashanti 22%; Western 9%; Central 6%; Eastern 6%; Brong-Ahafo 4%; Volta 4%; Northern 4%; Upper East/West 3%. Regional sub-indices are published in the quarterly CIPI Technical Supplement available to subscribers.
